Phoenix Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitor Project
Sub-project: OptoElectronic Sensor, Phoenix Project team

Lab Notes, 21 November 2009

Attendees:
Sathish Thiruvengadam
Larry Beaty
Steven James
Chase Turner

Notes

We powered up Garrett's IR circuit and quite surprisingly were able to obtain signals on fingers instantly without much tuning.  We tried to understand the delay between two channels placed on different fingers.  Chase suggested checking signals at different locations such as ear, neck, cheeks, wrist, mid arm and fingers.  Not much luck in getting any signals except on fingers.  Irrespective of fingers (Index, middle, ring and pinky), we found some time delay (4ms and 10 ms) between two fingers.  We thought, signal on index finger would lead signal on middle finger.  Perhaps, signal on different fingers vary with respect to different individuals (tried on me, Steven and Chase).  Found signals on mid arm and wrist with varying time difference (76 ms and 16 ms).  However, we noticed that signal on wrist was leading mid arm.  Time difference between signals from channel 1 and 2 on same finger was close to zero.  We compared the time difference from wrist and mid arm with past work by another Phoenix group.  Found time difference on their work on Pieozo film was approximately 40 ms.  


Steven suggested to replace the 5 V DC power supply with a 5 V triangular wave signal @ 1 Hz to study the time differences between two channels.    Found that signal on channel 2 led channel 1 by 4 ms.  We suspect that this difference would be caused by the circuit components.

Larry asked Germaine and Othild (in the chronobiology department) if there was a potential physiological reason for time differences in the signals across fingers, and the answer was essentially "nothing that we know of."



Plans:

Look for any information reported on IR sensors used for blood pressure monitor and suggested time delay between signals.

Learn about other non-invasive blood pressure monitor techniques.
